AI and the Product Manager

March 2026 · 5 min read

AI is not a feature. It's an inflection point — and product managers are uniquely positioned to lead organizations through it.

When the internet went mainstream in the 1990s, the companies that thrived weren't necessarily the ones with the best technology. They were the ones with leaders who understood what the technology made possible and built products around that understanding. Web 2.0 brought a second wave — participation, platforms, network effects — and again, the winners were those who saw the shift coming and oriented their organizations around it early.

We're in that moment again. AI — particularly the large language model wave and the agentic systems being built on top of it — represents a fundamental change in what software can do. Not an incremental improvement. A step change.

What Makes This Moment Different

Previous software automation was deterministic. You wrote rules; the system followed them. You could trace every output back to an explicit instruction. That model has limits: the world is messier than any rule set you can write, and the cost of encoding every edge case is prohibitive.

AI operates on patterns and probabilities. It generalizes from examples rather than executing instructions. That means it can handle ambiguity, infer intent, and operate effectively in domains where formal rules are hard to define — natural language, image interpretation, complex decision-making under uncertainty. The ceiling of what's automatable has risen dramatically.

For product managers, this changes the question from "should we build this feature?" to "what should software do now that it couldn't before?"

The PM's Advantage

Engineers understand what the models can do technically. Executives understand the strategic pressure to adopt. But product managers sit at the intersection of user needs, business goals, and technical capability — which is exactly where the hard work of AI product development happens.

The failure mode I see most often is organizations treating AI as a solution looking for a problem. They integrate a chatbot, add a "generate with AI" button, and call it a roadmap. The result is underwhelming — because the technology wasn't matched to a genuine pain point.

The PM's job is to resist that. To stay grounded in the problem space. To ask: what do our users actually struggle with? Where does the cost of human judgment or human labor create a bottleneck? What would they do differently if that bottleneck didn't exist? Those are the seams where AI belongs.

What I've Learned Building AI Products

Across my career in mineral processing and healthcare, I've built and shipped AI-powered tools in high-stakes environments. A few things I've found to be consistently true:

  • Data readiness is the real constraint. The model is rarely the bottleneck. What holds teams back is the quality, structure, and accessibility of the underlying data. Getting data AI-usable is a product problem, not just a data engineering problem.
  • Trust is earned incrementally. Users don't adopt AI tools all at once. They probe the edges, look for failures, and calibrate their trust over time. Design for that. Give users visibility into how recommendations are made. Build feedback loops. Make it easy to override.
  • The right metric is outcomes, not adoption. It's easy to celebrate usage numbers. It's harder — and more important — to measure whether the AI is actually improving decisions. Define that upfront, before you ship.
  • Iteration speed matters more than initial accuracy. An AI product with an 80% hit rate that ships and improves is better than a 95% accuracy model that takes two years to reach production. Get something in users' hands and learn from real behavior.

Where I'm Focused

My current work at Phreesia is centered on enabling data to be AI-usable at scale in healthcare. That means thinking carefully about data structure, governance, and the feedback loops that let AI systems improve over time in a domain where accuracy and trust are non-negotiable.

It also means staying close to the practitioners — the people in healthcare practices making decisions every day — to understand where AI can genuinely lighten their load and where it would introduce friction or risk.

The organizations that will lead the next decade aren't the ones with the biggest models. They're the ones with the clearest sense of what they're trying to accomplish, and the product discipline to build toward it.

That's the work I find most energizing. And I think PMs are at the center of it.
